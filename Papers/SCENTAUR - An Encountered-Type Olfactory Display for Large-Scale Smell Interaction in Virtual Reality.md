## Introduction

>“Replicating olfactory stimuli presents considerable challenges due to the complexity of scent perception, which lacks a universally accepted encoding method [Bar16]. Furthermore, the inherently subjective experience of smell, potential side effects such as smell adaptation [BBEL71], and the often bulky and cumbersome nature of current olfactory displays [GRKRM21]” (Brument et al., 2025, p. 2)

>“Encountered-Type Olfactory Display (ETOD) approach, inspired by Encountered-Type Haptic Displays (ETHD)” (Brument et al., 2025, p. 2)

>“pleasant scents have been shown to reduce stress and promote positive emotions” (Brument et al., 2025, p. 3) [[Virtual reality and stimulation of touch and smell for inducing relaxation - A randomized controlled trial]]

>“Regarding unpleasant odors, they can increase the realism and sense of presence compared to neutral odors” (Brument et al., 2025, p. 3) [[In a dirty virtual room - exposure to an unpleasant odor increases the senses of presence, reality, and realism]]

>“Another factor that can increase presence in VR is how well users can identify smells [PD20b], where consistency between visual and olfactory feedback is fundamental.” (Brument et al., 2025, p. 3)

>“Last, the perception of odor intensity and directionality can be modulated by virtual odor representation” (Brument et al., 2025, p. 3) [[Does Virtual Odor Representation Influence the Perception of Olfactory Intensity and Directionality in VR]]

>“Related work showed that smell intensity [TTP∗21] and the type of smell can influence smell perception [PD20b]” (Brument et al., 2025, p. 5)
## Protocol design

>“To investigate participants’ smell perceptions and interaction with SCENTAUR, our experiment had a three Smell Intensity (200, 400, 600) x three Smell Type (Rose, Wood, Grass). Smell Intensity is referred to as low, medium, and high intensity values recommended by the Olorama documentation.” (Brument et al., 2025, p. 5)

>“We set the fan duration as a constant of 4 seconds, which was recommended as a value from the Olorama constructor for our study” (Brument et al., 2025, p. 5)
>“The experiment comprised three blocks corresponding to each Smell Intensity, where each Smell Type was tested once. Each block was counterbalanced using a Latin square design” (Brument et al., 2025, p. 5)

>“A total of 14 participants (7 males, 7 females, M = 29.5; SD = 4.15) participated in the experiment. They had no olfactory disorders” (Brument et al., 2025, p. 5)

>“Then, they filled out a pre Virtual Reality Sickness Questionnaire (VRSQ) to measure cybersickness” (Brument et al., 2025, p. 5)

>“Following the training, the actual experiment began” (Brument et al., 2025, p. 5)

>“Participants performed the navigation task (i.e., searching for three Spot Goals and smelling three different Smell Types with a given Smell Intensity). After completing the task, they filled out the post-block questionnaires, and we aired out the room for at least 5 minutes before repeating the navigation task to remove the smell from the previous block to minimize odor adaptation” (Brument et al., 2025, p. 5)

>“We repeated this procedure twice to test the two other Smell Intensities. Finally, participants completed the post-experiment questionnaire and were interviewed for additional feedback” (Brument et al., 2025, p. 5)
## Measures

>“Regarding subjective metrics, we recorded the smell intensity perceived and participant guesses per Smell Type. After the experiment, we collected participants’ subjective feedback with the following questions: "How much did you trust the VR system’s ability to simulate smells accurately? (1 - Did not trust at all, 7 - Trusted very much"); "How far were you (in cm) from the smell source during the experiment?". Participants could also report general comments about the study and the SCENTAUR system.” (Brument et al., 2025, p. 5)

## Results & discussion

>“To the question "How much did you trust the VR system’s ability to simulate smells accurately?", participants reported a score of 5.21±1.47 on a 7Likert scale.” (Brument et al., 2025, p. 7)

>“The soundscape, including birds chirping, was noted, although some participants mentioned being able to hear the robot, which slightly broke the immersion (participants 2, 4, 6)” (Brument et al., 2025, p. 7)

>“they sometimes had trouble identifying exactly what the scents were (participants 10, 11, 13)” (Brument et al., 2025, p. 7)

>“noted that one scent was much stronger than the others (participant 11” (Brument et al., 2025, p. 7)

>“the Smell Intensity resulted in different times to detect the smell, where the lowest was with the highest intensity” (Brument et al., 2025, p. 7)

>“depending on the scenario, the intensity could be lower if time to detect the smell is not a priority” (Brument et al., 2025, p. 7)

>“Several users associated the Flower smell with something floral or sweet, such as perfume, lavender, or roses, and generally found it pleasant and recognizable (users 3, 5, 8, 9” (Brument et al., 2025, p. 7)

>“Some, however, described it as reminiscent of toilet spray or overly artificial (user 6)” (Brument et al., 2025, p. 7)

>“The Grass was described as grassy, nature-like, or refreshing, though harder to define (users 3, 5, 8, 10, 12). Participants used comparisons like “wet flowers,” “grasses,” or “salty hay” to describe it.” (Brument et al., 2025, p. 7)

>“The Wood scent drew more negative feedback, often described as intense, spicy, woody, or even uncomfortable—something participants wouldn’t want to be exposed to for extended periods (users 4, 5, 6, 8, 10)” (Brument et al., 2025, p. 7)
# Synthesis

This study tries to find if [[Scent]]s delivered by a robot have better results that typical [[Olfactory]] devices.
There were only 14 participants. They found that too intense scents had a negative impact on [[Pleasantness judgment]] and identification.
In their protocol, they also made sure to avoid [[Odor adaptation]]
