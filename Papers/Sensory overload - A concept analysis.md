# Introduction

>“In some situations (e.g., fatigue or increased stress) many people experience the phenomenon of sensory overload insofar ‘that even secondary stimuli cannot be cut out and permanently distract, irritate and disturb’ (Sullwold 1991” (Scheydt et al., 2017, p. 110)

>“Especially in people with a diagnosis of certain mental illnesses (e.g., schizophrenia, ADHD or dementia), the problem of sensory overload due to a disturbed stimulus filtering appears to occur more frequently (Perry et al. 2007; Quednow 2008).” (Scheydt et al., 2017, p. 110)
# Objective

>“The main objective of the present concept analysis is the clarification and analysis of the meaning and inner structure of the concept sensory overload and its use in the psychiatric context” (Scheydt et al., 2017, p. 111)
# Concept

>“Sensory overload is predominantly described in conjunction with schizophrenia, attention deficit hyperactivity disorders (ADHD) or autism” (Scheydt et al., 2017, p. 113)

>“sensory overload, sensory deprivation, sensory distortion, and sensory monotony are special forms of disturbances of sensory perception (Roy 1991).” (Scheydt et al., 2017, p. 113)

>“sensory overload is rooted in an impaired cognitive capacity to process information resulting in a reduction in the stimulus level and thus rendering subordinate stimuli to be experienced as aversive or burdensome” (Scheydt et al., 2017, p. 114)

>“‘increase in pulse and respiration frequency, in blood pressure, in confusion, in anxiety, in mental stress and/or making mistakes’ (Venes 2009), as well as (iii) a perceived reduction of the meaningfulness of the environment” (Scheydt et al., 2017, p. 115)

>“the concept of sensory overload or overstimulation may be defined as subjectively or objectively perceived and as atypical or excessive stimulation that exceeds an individual’s usual level. This entails, in other words, that the individual perceives an increase in the intensity, diversity, and/or the patterns of environmental stimuli which exceed the normally experienced level and are thus experienced as aversive” (Scheydt et al., 2017, p. 115)
# Consequences

>“The first reaction of an individual to such a stressful event is the endeavour to restore sensoristasis by routine behaviour. Such reactions may be understood as ‘alarm reactions’ which may be observed in terms of physiological stress reactions and/or situation-specific coping attempts as well as mechanisms of selfprotection” (Scheydt et al., 2017, p. 116)

>"On failing to restore sensoristasis an individual may resort to mechanisms such as secondary appraisal (cf. Lazarus & Folkman 1994) or cognitive coping mechanisms (cf. Roy & Andrews 1991) to elicit coping attitudes and resources” (Scheydt et al., 2017, p. 116)

>“If sensory overload persists over a longer period of time it is possible that existing symptoms are amplified” (Scheydt et al., 2017, p. 117)

# Synthesis

This paper tries to define sensory overload, for nursing contexts.
First sensory overload seems to be characteristic of schyzophrenia, [[ADHD]], [[ASD]], disorders like bipolar disorder or even dementia
Basically, sensory overload is defined as the mental state when one feels like stimuli are aversive or burdensome. It's always provoked by stimuli's intensity, surprise or amount and can induce stress, confusion, anxiety or amount of mistakes and overall irritation or disturbance.
Also, sensory overload can be avoided by coping mechanisms or fleeing stimuli, but a stimuli-deprived environment can still be considered not ideal for the individual suffering from overload.
