# Efficient coding and adaptation

>Barlow’s efficient coding hypothesis suggests that, given a finite capacity to transmit information, neural systems employ an optimally efficient coding strategy to represent the inputs that they typically process. However, when collected over relatively short time or length scales, the local statistics of many natural stimuli differ greatly from their global distribution. For example, luminance and contrast in natural visual environments vary over orders of magnitude across time in a day or across space in a complex scene

>the timescale for the change in coding strategy cannot be shorter than the time required for the system to ‘‘learn’’ the new distribution

# Systems adapt to a variety of stimulus statistics

>The simplest instantiation of a stimulus probability distribution is one in which the stimulus takes one of two possible values. If one of these values is presented more frequently than the other, a system may adapt to give a stronger response to the rarer stimulus

>A simple model for natural stimuli is of local Gaussian fluctuations with a long-tailed mean and variance modulated on longer time or length scales

# Timescales

>In tracking changing stimulus statistics, there are two relevant timescales for any system: the characteristic timescale of changes in the stimulus distribution and the minimum time required by an ideal observer to estimate the parameters of the new distribution. The first timescale is established by the environment, while the second is determined by statistics and sets a lower bound on how quickly any adapting system could estimate parameters of the new distribution

>As discussed above, the first of these components rapidly rescales the system’s input-output gain following a change in stimulus statistics. The second component of adaptation dynamics is a slow change in the system’s mean firing rate

### **Seconds**

- Rapid light adaptation
    
- Acoustic reflex in the middle ear
    
- Touch receptor desensitization
### **Minutes to hours**

- Dark adaptation
    
- Taste and smell sensitivity changes
    
- Motion adaptation (e.g., waterfall illusion)
### **Days to years**

- Long-term recalibration of sensory maps (e.g., prism goggles experiments)
    
- Cross-modal plasticity (blind individuals enhancing audition/touch)

## Auto-Synthesis

The authors conclude that:

- Sensory systems dynamically reconfigure their coding strategies across a wide range of timescales.
    
- These adaptations improve **information transmission** under changing stimulus conditions.
    
- Some forms of adaptation that appear to be complex or “learning-like” may arise from simpler **intrinsic nonlinearities**.
    
- Sensory processing cannot be treated as a static feed-forward filter; instead, it is deeply shaped by the **statistics of natural stimuli** and by **adaptive plasticity at multiple levels** of neural processing.
