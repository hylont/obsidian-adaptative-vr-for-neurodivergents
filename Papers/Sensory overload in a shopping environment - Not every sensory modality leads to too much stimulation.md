# Introduction

>“Spence et al. (2014), however, warns that, due to too much arousal, introducing more cues into a store environment may trigger sensory overload” (Doucé and Adams, 2020, p. 1)

>“Sensory overload refers to a situation where individuals are unable to process the input stimuli, leading to negative consequences like confusion, frustration and withdrawal (Malhotra, 1984).” (Doucé and Adams, 2020, p. 2)

>“congruent combinations of two high arousal atmospheric cues (e.g., a red background color combined with fast music) led to positive consumer reactions, whereas the presence of three high arousal cues (i.e., red background color, grapefruit scent and fast music) had negative effects.” (Doucé and Adams, 2020, p. 2)
# Objective

>“the aim of this paper is to test whether the likelihood of sensory overload occurring is heightened by a) the order in which high arousal sensorial cues are being added as well as b) the type of sensorial cues being added to the store environment. Based on Homburg et al. (2012), we expect that adding a third high arousal cue to a store environment triggers sensory overload which will lead to (a) more arousal experienced, (b) less pleasure experienced, (c) lower levels of approach behavior, and (d) more negative evaluations.” (Doucé and Adams, 2020, p. 3)
# Experimental design

## Study 1

>“Study 1 was conducted in a simulated grocery store constructed in a retail lab (approximately 57 square meter)” (Doucé and Adams, 2020, p. 4)

>“This manipulation resulted in a between-subjects design with four arousal conditions: (1) low arousal light, (2) high arousal light, (3) high arousal light and high arousal ambient scent, and (4) high arousal light, high arousal ambient scent, and high arousal music.” (Doucé and Adams, 2020, p. 4)

>“The correlated color temperature of the light setting was either 3000 K (more yellow warm light) or 4000 K (more bluish cold light), whereas the illuminance level ranged between 305 lux (dim light) and 657 lux (bright light). The warm, dim light setting (3000 K and 305 lux) was chosen as the low arousal light condition, whereas the cold, bright light setting (4000 K and 657 lux) was chosen as the high arousal light condition.” (Doucé and Adams, 2020, p. 4)

>“From an earlier pretest with 25 students evaluating 46 scents, we selected five scents that scored high on arousal (i.e., lemon, bergamot, mint, kiwi, and red berries).” (Doucé and Adams, 2020, p. 4)

>“Between aromas participants smelled coffee grounds to restore their scent palettes (Secundo and Sobel, 2006). This technique is frequently used in the fragrance industry to neutralize the odors in the nose” (Doucé and Adams, 2020, p. 4)

>“Due to the highest score on arousal of bergamot, we selected bergamot as the high arousal scent.” (Doucé and Adams, 2020, p. 4)

>“Twenty participants were asked (1) whether they noted something special in the lab, and (2) when we mentioned the presence of a scent, whether they noticed the scent (Douc ́e and Janssens, 2013). Spontaneously mentioning the scent when asked the first question meant that the scent was too salient. The intensity of the scent was lowered until participants answered negatively to the first question and positively to the second.” (Doucé and Adams, 2020, p. 4)

# Results

>“consumers experience more arousal when exposed to three high arousal cues than when exposed to one low arousal cue, one high arousal cue as well as two high arousal cues” (Doucé and Adams, 2020, p. 5)

>“consumers experience less pleasure in a store environment with three high arousal atmospheric cues compared to an environment with only one low arousal cue, one high arousal cue and two high arousal cues” (Doucé and Adams, 2020, p. 5)

>“In sum, when a higher sense cue is added as third high arousal atmospheric cue, the characteristics referring to a possible state of sensory overload (i.e., increase in arousal and decrease in pleasure) are found.” (Doucé and Adams, 2020, p. 5)


>“The aim of Study 1 and Study 2 was to examine sensory overload by adding a third high arousal atmospheric cue in the store environment in a well-controlled laboratory environment. While in each study three senses (i.e., vision, audition and olfaction) were triggered, the order in which these senses were added to the store environment, however, differed. In particular, the third high arousal cue used to trigger sensory overload was either a cue processed by a higher sense (i.e., music in Study 1) or a cue processed by a lower sense (i.e., ambient scent in Study 2).” (Doucé and Adams, 2020, p. 7)

>“Results indicated that sensory overload is triggered by adding a third higher sense cue, but not by adding a third lower sense cue.” (Doucé and Adams, 2020, p. 7)


# Conclusion

>“To summarize, we found that adding extra high arousal cues to the store environment results in more experienced arousal but the overload due to high arousal can only be found when the third cue added is a cue perceived by a higher sense (i.e., auditory sense in Study 1 and visual sense in Study 3). In this case, three high arousal cues lead to less pleasure and less approach behavior than only one low arousal cue, one high arousal cue or two high arousal cues.” (Doucé and Adams, 2020, p. 10)

>“First, the results of Study 1 and Study 3 indicate that perceived pleasure remained steady with the adding of cues until the final third high arousal cue was added which resulted in a significant drop in perceived pleasantness while the perceived arousal significantly kept on rising with the addition of cues. The emergence of this pattern seems to be independent of type of store since this patterns was found for a grocery store (Study 1) as well as a fashion store (Study 3).” (Doucé and Adams, 2020, p. 10)

>“no sensory overload was created by adding a third cue processed by a lower sense compared to a third cue processed by a higher sense” (Doucé and Adams, 2020, p. 10)

>“in low target-arousal contexts (e.g., library) increasing the level of arousal will not be accepted and will consequently negatively influence the perceived pleasure, the authors found that in high target-arousal contexts (e.g., amusement parks) increasing the level of arousal will enhance perceived pleasure. In other words, the optimal level of stimulation may, besides differ between people, also differ between contexts. I” (Doucé and Adams, 2020, p. 10)

>“Furthermore, information processing and attention can also be negatively influenced when there is too much stimulation or arousal” (Doucé and Adams, 2020, p. 10)

>“the momentum where sensory overload could take place is influenced by the number of cues as well as the type of cues being added. Higher senses of sight and audition are more dominant and attract more attention than lower senses like olfaction, leading to a higher chance of sensory overload to occur” (Doucé and Adams, 2020, p. 10)

>“to avoid sensory overload to occur, the best strategy is to not add any high arousal cues to an environment” (Doucé and Adams, 2020, p. 11)
